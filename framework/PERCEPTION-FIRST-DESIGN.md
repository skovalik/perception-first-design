## The Bouncer

I figured out my design methodology in a nightclub.

I was 21, working nights at a nightclub in Santa Barbara to help pay for school at UCSB. Started as a barback. After about a month, my boss pulled me aside during a rush and chewed me out for not working hard enough. He'd caught one snapshot of me standing still. Didn't matter that I'd been busing my ass all night. He saw what he saw.

I waited until the end of the night, asked for five minutes in his office, and said: "I get why you saw what you saw and reacted the way you did. But that moment wasn't representative of what I'd been doing. I get paid to do a job, and I'll do it well." The next night, he asked me if I wanted to learn how to check IDs and become the second security manager on staff. That promotion was the first time I consciously identified a perception gap: his impression didn't match reality, and my response addressed the perception, not the facts.

The nightclub held about 300 people. Every night at 1:30 AM, same problem: lights slam on, DJ announces we're closed, and I'm barking at 300 drunk people to get out. They hated it. I hated it. Fights broke out. Stragglers lingered. The whole thing was hostile.

So I tried something different. At 1:10, I walked table by table. "Hey, last call's coming up. Get any drinks you want now. Thanks for coming out tonight." That's it. No urgency. No authority voice. Just information, a suggestion, and respect.

At 1:30, when the DJ made the announcement and the lights went up, half the venue was already heading for the door. No barking. No fights. And bar sales went up 30% in those last 20 minutes.

I didn't know what to call that. I just knew it worked, and I knew *why* it worked. The old way treated people like obstacles. My way treated them like people with their own goals who'd cooperate if you gave them the right information at the right time in the right tone.

Working the door also taught me things about my own neurology I wouldn't name for years. Eye contact was overwhelming for me, like staring into the sun, but I could see that other people extracted meaning from it. So I studied it systematically. I used to ask people how they were doing, literally, until someone told me I should stop because they could be having a terrible day. I switched to telling people I hoped they were having a good night. Small change. People's attention turned on. The greeting became an activation point before I had that vocabulary.

That's Perception-First Design before I had words for it. Everything I've done in the 15 years since, revenue tripling, Costco pallets and Walmart shelves and Disney licensing, improv theaters and smart home brands. It all comes from that same instinct. Design for how people actually perceive and process the world, not how you wish they did.

## The Thesis

The design industry has spent 15 years saying the same thing: users have limited attention, so don't waste it. Lower friction. Reduce steps. Don't make them think.

My version is different: **users don't think, until you make them.**

Nobody is thinking. They're on autopilot. The same way you catch a ball without calculating trajectory, or walk without planning each step, or explain something when asked a question without scripting your answer first — that's how people interact with websites, products, brands, everything. Autopilot. Energy conservation. The brain handles the vast majority of processing unconsciously — what Kahneman (2011) calls System 1, the fast, automatic mode that runs most of cognitive life. System 2 — slow, deliberate, conscious — only activates when something demands attention. If we had to think about everything, we'd go nuts.

Contemporary cognitive science calls this **predictive processing** (Clark, 2013; Friston, 2010). Friston's free energy principle provides the mathematical neuroscience foundation — the brain minimizes prediction error to maintain internal models of the world. Clark's treatment makes this actionable as a design framework: predictions that match generate fluent, automatic processing; violations generate attention. The brain is constantly generating predictions about what it expects to encounter. When reality matches prediction — autopilot. When reality violates prediction — attention fires, System 2 kicks in. That prediction error is what I call the **activation point.**

The design challenge isn't "don't waste their limited attention." It's that effortful attention is **dormant by default** — the brain is always processing, but conscious engagement is off until something activates it. The real question is: when you do make them stop and actually think — when you generate a prediction error that activates them from autopilot — what do they think about? How do they feel? And what do they do next?

That's what I design for. Not the funnel. The activation.

| | The Industry Says | I Say |
|---|---|---|
| **Assumption** | Attention is scarce — conserve it | Effortful attention is dormant — design the activation |
| **Goal** | Reduce friction to goals | Create the right moments that make someone stop, think, and act |
| **Failure mode** | User runs out of patience | User never engages — autopilots right past you |
| **Metaphor** | Budget of attention | Autopilot with activation points |

Steve Krug wrote *Don't Make Me Think.* Great book — it gave me the vocabulary to talk to stakeholders early in my career. But the reality is more nuanced than "reduce friction." The reality is: nobody's thinking in the first place. Design for *that* bias — the bias of making someone stop and engage — instead of funneling them to an action with an affordance of attention they never gave you.

## Why I See This

Two things about my brain that I didn't choose but that turned out to be professional advantages.

I'm autistic. After my formal ASD diagnosis, my partner pointed out something I'd been missing: "Do you realize you're not picking up on me not being interested at all in what you're talking about, and you seem to be getting frustrated?" Around 2012, a friend I met at a Chicago meetup about emotions in video games suggested I try improv. I went in already analytical. I had impaired social instincts, not absent ones. I didn't need to learn what a smile means. I had to learn what eye contact can mean, what a pause signals, what "said vs. unsaid" reveals. I learned it like you'd learn an instrument or ballet or bodybuilding: theory, practice, application loops, over and over.

That means I can *see and articulate* the unconscious patterns that most designers apply intuitively but can't explain. People interact with websites the same way they interact with other humans. A review on a site carries the same weight as a friend's recommendation. We form first impressions on products the same way we do when someone walks into a room. We think and feel things about brands as if they were a person we know. It's all unconscious, and we can't help it.

I also have severe combined-type ADHD. Some experiences are literally painful for me because of how taxing they are on my attention and cognitive load. I'm acutely sensitive to friction, clutter, noise, confusion — the things that bad design inflicts on everyone but that I feel at a higher volume. I design because I'm sensitive to these patterns. What most designers theorize about, I feel.

And the ADHD gave me something else: years of building mental prostheses. If it's out of sight, it's literally out of mind for me — so I keep post-it notes on my desk for today's tasks. I have to actively "turn on" when listening to people, processing all the non-verbal cues and implications and context that neurotypical people handle automatically. That active listening is a learned skill, like a dancer controlling their body on stage.

Those prostheses map directly to design. "Out of sight, out of mind" becomes: what MUST we show vs. what we want to show vs. what we could show — then cut everything but what we must show. Active listening becomes: who is this for, what's at stake, what's the desired outcome, what's being said and not said. The same scaffolding I build for my own brain is the scaffolding I build for users.

Design is prosthesis for human cognitive functions and limitations. I know that because I've been building prostheses for myself my whole life. The curb cut effect: what I built for my own cognitive accessibility turned out to help everyone.

## The Conductor Model

I don't build funnels. I conduct.

Think about a musician who's reached unconscious mastery — they stop thinking about how things are done and focus entirely on how the music makes you feel. That's the goal. Prime someone with a first impression, then conduct their thoughts and emotions through the experience. Design a mental waterfall.

There's a flow of thought and perception: momentum, weight, cadence, tone, speed, context, priming, expectations. Like singing down a scale in front of a crowd and pausing before the last note — everyone knows what comes next. That recognition, that pattern completion, is satisfying. That's what a great CTA feels like: the only possible resolution to the experience that preceded it.

The bouncer story is the conductor model in action. I primed each table (personal visit). I set the tempo (1:10, 20 minutes before close). I gave them a clear next action (get your drinks). I resolved the experience (thanks for coming). By the time the lights came up, leaving felt natural — not forced.

A website works the same way:

**The User's Mental Waterfall** — the unconscious sequence of questions every visitor processes:

1. **What am I trying to accomplish here?** → Intent
2. **Is there a clear path to that?** → Navigation
3. **What's the point of this site?** → Value proposition
4. **For whom is this?** → Do I belong here?
5. **What is it trying to tell me?** → Messaging — *ew* or *cool?*
6. **What is it trying to get me to do?** → Call to action
7. **How do I feel about doing it?** → "Prove you're worth my time" or "Tell me more!"
8. **Where's the win-win?** → Mutual value
9. **Does this give me what I want?** → Delivery

The hero section is the activation point — the table visit at 1:10. If it answers questions 1 through 5 in the first second or two, the user is primed. Then you conduct them through 6 through 9 as they scroll. If the hero fails, it's the lights slamming on. They're gone before you've started.

My improv teacher gave me a formula for this: **(Listen) × (Act + React)**. That's the formula for being in the moment. If Listen equals zero — if the user isn't attending — then everything else multiplies by zero. The first impression IS the moment that activates listening. Everything depends on it landing.

## The Two Layers

When I design for different demographics, here's what changes and what stays the same.

**What stays the same: the cognitive architecture.** We all use the same machinery to perceive the world. Attention, memory, pattern recognition, energy conservation, first impressions, mind perception, the autopilot default — these are human universals. The architecture is shared — but the parameters vary. Processing speed, working memory capacity, visual acuity, and contrast sensitivity all decline with normal aging (Salthouse, 1996; Owsley, 2011). This strengthens PFD rather than weakening it: design for the tightest constraints, and you design well for everyone. The 22-year-old gets a faster, smoother version of the same experience. The fundamentals are predictive because they're universal — but the design should be calibrated to the floor, not the ceiling.

**What changes: the software.** Different demographics have different wants, needs, desires, avoidances. Different cognitive budgets — not just money, but patience, trust, attention. Different shared cultural moments, different levels of digital literacy, different expectations about what a "trustworthy" site looks like. Styling is the biggest variable — what's in vogue, what resonates with nostalgia, what a group "expects" or aspires toward.

All rules are made up. When you know what they are, you can break or bend or go around them. Cultural conventions are game rules built on cognitive physics. I work on the physics. Then I customize the game rules through empathy modeling — embodying the traits and context of who I'm designing for to get directionality on what'd work.

The methodology is portable across industries and cultures because it's grounded in the hardware layer. The customization happens on the software layer.

## The Framework

Perception-First Design has five layers. They work as a dependency stack — each one depends on the layer below it. Fix them in order, because upstream failures significantly degrade everything downstream.

But think of it less as a stack and more as a composition. You're conducting an experience. The foundation sets the key. Each layer adds another instrument. If the foundation is broken, the whole piece is noise.

```
                    ┌─────────────────────────┐
                    │   Decision Architecture  │  ← Layer 4: Build the trail
                    ├─────────────────────────┤
                    │  Perception Bias         │  ← Layer 3: Feel, not features
                    │  Optimization            │
                    ├─────────────────────────┤
                    │  Processing Fluency      │  ← Layer 2: Rhythm and consistency
                    ├─────────────────────────┤
                    │  First-Impression        │  ← Layer 1: The activation point
                    │  Architecture            │
                    ├─────────────────────────┤
                    │  Cognitive Load           │  ← Foundation (L0): Clear the bandwidth
                    │  Reduction               │
                    └─────────────────────────┘
```

This maps to how the brain actually processes visual information — from the eyes through successive stages of visual processing, with each stage constrained by the bandwidth of the stage below it. Graphemes before letters before words before sentences before paragraphs before meaning. You can't process the message if you can't process the words.

In predictive processing terms (Clark, 2013; Hohwy, 2013): when a design matches the brain's predictions at every layer, processing is fluid and unconscious — autopilot. When a layer fails, it generates prediction error that demands conscious attention. Good design means each layer confirms predictions so the brain's resources flow toward the decisions that matter, not toward deciphering the interface.

**A note on the research base:** The empirical benchmarks throughout this framework — timing thresholds, working memory parameters, fluency and priming effects — were measured primarily on WEIRD samples (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic; Henrich, Heine & Norenzayan, 2010). The underlying cognitive architecture is expected to generalize across populations; specific parameter values may vary. PFD is a practitioner synthesis, not an experimental program — the citations establish the mechanisms; the 15-year applied record tests them.

### Foundation (L0): Cognitive Load Reduction

> "Can't perceive anything without the bandwidth to do so."

The brain is an energy optimization machine. Working memory holds about 3-5 chunks at a time — not the "7 plus or minus 2" you've seen quoted everywhere. Miller (1956) was actually describing the recurrence of the number 7 across disparate information-processing phenomena — channel capacity, absolute judgment, memory span — as a rhetorical observation, not proposing a single working memory limit. That nuance got lost. Cowan (2001, 2010) established the modern estimate through controlled experiments: roughly 3-5 chunks when rehearsal and grouping strategies are prevented. When processing demands exceed that capacity, the brain defaults to the easiest action available: leaving.

Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory (1988) makes a useful distinction: **intrinsic load** is the complexity inherent to the task (comparing insurance plans is harder than picking a t-shirt size), and **extraneous load** is complexity caused by poor design (confusing navigation, unnecessary fields, competing elements). You can't change intrinsic load — the task is what it is. But every unit of extraneous load you eliminate frees working memory for the decisions that actually matter.

This is the foundation because without bandwidth, nothing else works. An overloaded visitor can't form a first impression, can't process your brand, can't connect with your message, can't find the trail. They're just gone.

**In practice:**
- Reduce choices at every step. Audit every dropdown, radio button, and "which one?" moment.
- Progressive disclosure — show what's needed now, reveal the rest on demand.
- Smart defaults — pre-select what 80% of users choose.
- If you don't need a form field to complete the transaction, delete it. If you need it later, ask later.

**How I know it's failing:** Users abandon forms halfway. High bounce on pages with many options. People call support for basic tasks. "Where do I click?" feedback.

**Simply Smart Home example:** The site presented "very matter-of-fact statements and not much else." Users had to work to figure out what the product did or why they'd want it. I restructured content to answer user questions before they had to ask them. Freed up bandwidth so they could evaluate the product instead of deciphering the interface.

### Layer 1: First-Impression Architecture

> "Your site has 50 milliseconds. That's not a design constraint. That's the physics of how your visitors' brains work."

Visual evaluation begins within the first 50 milliseconds — faster than conscious thought (Lindgaard et al., 2006; Tuch et al., 2012). Tractinsky, Cokhavi, Kirschenbaum & Sharfi (2006) confirmed that aesthetic judgments at brief exposures are consistent and reliable, reinforcing the original finding. Reinecke et al. (2013) at Google Research found effects at exposures as short as 17ms — even faster than Lindgaard's already-striking threshold. The follow-up literature has debated what exactly gets evaluated that fast: aesthetic appeal, visual complexity, or some combination. The functional implication for design is the same either way — the brain makes a go/no-go evaluation before conscious processing engages. Whether it's "this looks good" or "this looks simple enough to process," the design either passes or it doesn't. Everything downstream depends on clearing this gate.

The aesthetic-usability effect (Kurosu & Kashimura, 1995; replicated cross-culturally by Tractinsky, 1997) shows that attractive interfaces are perceived as more usable, even with identical functionality. Beautiful things are perceived as more functional. This isn't vanity — it's psychology. The effect has boundary conditions: it attenuates when usability failures are severe enough to override aesthetics, and repeated use can erode it. But for first encounters and moderate interactions — which is what most web design targets — the effect is robust.

People form first impressions on products the same way they do when someone walks into a room. A review on your site carries weight like a friend's recommendation. We construct lightweight models of competence and care for websites, brands, and products the same way we do for strangers — not because we literally believe websites have minds, but because the brain's social cognition systems activate automatically on anything that exhibits enough behavioral complexity. Heider & Simmel (1944) showed people attribute intentions, emotions, and goals to geometric shapes — triangles "chasing," circles "hiding." Gray, Gray & Wegner (2007) formalized this as **mind perception**: a spectrum with two dimensions — Agency (capacity to think/act) and Experience (capacity to feel/sense). It's not full Theory of Mind. It's enough to pass or fail the same trust evaluation we run on humans.

**The first impression IS the activation point.** It's the bouncer's table visit. It's the moment the user's attention switches from OFF to ON. If it fails, Listen equals zero, and everything multiplies by zero.

**In practice:**
- The hero section is the thesis statement of the entire site. If the thesis is wrong, everything below it fails.
- Faces in hero images exploit two well-documented attention mechanisms: neonatal face preference, present at birth (Johnson et al., 1991), and adult face "pop-out" in visual search, where faces are detected faster than other objects regardless of where they appear in a scene (Hershler & Hochstein, 2005).
- Visual quality must match price point. $20-40 tablets in plastic cases? I created the perception of "smart home decor" and sold them for $100-200. Perceived value meant more than anything else sales or marketing tried.
- The squint test: blur your eyes. If you can't get a sense for what's scannable, something's off with what you're trying to communicate.

**How I know it's failing:** High bounce with very low time-on-site. "It looks sketchy" feedback. Users describe the site as "cheap." Competitors with worse products are winning.

**iO Theater example:** Hobbyist WordPress site. Cluttered, inconsistent, Google Maps in the hero. "Doesn't look like a real theater." I rebuilt it with dramatic photography, clear event navigation, Bill Murray quote for social proof. Online ticket sales went from 50% to 75%. Same shows. Same prices. Different perception.

### Layer 2: Processing Fluency

> "If it's easy to process, it feels true."

This is one of the most robust findings in social cognition, though like all psychological effects, the specifics matter more than the headline. Reber & Schwarz (1999) showed that identical statements in easy-to-read fonts are judged as more *true* than the same statements in hard-to-read fonts — though font-specific manipulations show variable effect sizes across replications. The broader pattern is well-established: Alter & Oppenheimer (2009) showed that processing fluency effects generalize across truth, confidence, liking, and trust. Dechêne et al. (2010) confirmed the illusory truth effect — that repeated exposure to statements increases their perceived truth — across a meta-analysis of 51 studies, with processing fluency as the proposed mediating mechanism. The takeaway for design: fluent processing biases toward positive evaluation. The stronger claim — that fluency alone determines truth judgments — overstates it.

Think about it — if you're over 40 and can't read small grey text, how does that impact your experience? Typography, spacing, visual rhythm — these aren't decorative choices. They're the perception pipeline. If basic visual processing fails at the grapheme level, nothing above it works.

Processing fluency is why consistency matters more than creativity. We literally use cross-modal sensory words to describe everything — rough, coarse, soft, slippery, cold — for sounds, visuals, balance, not just touch. Lakoff & Johnson (1980) showed this isn't optional linguistic decoration — abstract thought is systematically structured by metaphors rooted in bodily experience. "HAPPY IS UP, SAD IS DOWN" reflects actual physical posture in those states. Spence (2011) demonstrated the perceptual reality: people consistently match high-pitched sounds with small, bright objects located high in space — cross-modal correspondences that are faster and more accurate when congruent. (*See What I'm Saying* by Lawrence Rosenblum is a practical demonstration of this.) We understand the world through embodied sensory metaphors, and when sensory input is consistent and fluid across channels, the brain reads it as trustworthy.

**In practice:**
- Typography rhythm: spacing cadence, visual flow. Not decorative — functional pacing of attention. This is the "music" layer of design.
- Unified branding across every touchpoint. Every inconsistency is a small processing fluency violation that compounds.
- 2 fonts maximum. 3-4 colors. Consistent spacing. Boring consistency beats exciting inconsistency.
- Color consistency operates in *perceptual* space, not physical space. Hex, RGB, and HSL are linear in physics but non-linear in perception — a CSS gradient that's "even" in hex is uneven to the eye. Near-miss color deviations (a brand blue that's 3% off) are disproportionately costly because they sit in the steep, high-sensitivity zone of the perceptual metric where prediction error is maximal (see Bujack et al., 2022 — *Accumulated Learnings* #16). Perceptually uniform spaces (OKLCH, CAM16-UCS) are L2 infrastructure.
- Visual quality consistent with price point. A $500 product on a $50 website creates dissonance the brain reads as "something is wrong here."

**How I know it's failing:** Inconsistent branding across touchpoints. Visual quality doesn't match the price. Multiple fonts and colors competing on the same page. The site looks different from the physical location or packaging.

**Simply Smart Home 4-year arc:** No brand system in 2020. By 2024, a comprehensive typography system, seasonal palettes, Disney and Brookstone sub-brand mood boards, ClicSmart and myLuma sub-brands — all with family resemblance. Processing fluency isn't a one-time fix. It's brand infrastructure that compounds. Each year, perceived quality matched price point more precisely. Price objections decreased. Vendors got more interested. Revenue 3x.

### Layer 3: Perception Bias Optimization

> "It's not for you. It's for them."

This is the most politically difficult principle. It means telling stakeholders that their instincts about their own customers may be wrong. I've had entire teams push back on decisions — until results proved the approach right. Every time.

The core insight: users autopilot decisions, then construct rational explanations after the fact. Nisbett & Wilson (1977) demonstrated this definitively — people are often unaware of what influenced their decisions and, when asked, generate plausible post-hoc rationalizations based on what they believe *should* have mattered. Kahneman (2011) formalized it: System 1 makes the decision; System 2 constructs the explanation. Survey data captures the System 2 rationalization. Analytics captures the System 1 decision. The gap between them is where perception problems hide.

In predictive processing terms (Clark, 2013): the brain constantly generates predictions about what it expects to encounter. When reality matches prediction — autopilot. When reality violates prediction — attention fires. The implication for this layer: design for what the brain *predicts*, not what users *report*. What users say in surveys is their System 2 reconstruction. What their brain predicted — and how the design matched or violated that prediction — is what actually drove behavior.

Ultimately what works in marketing isn't "here's what our product does" — it's "here's what having our product makes you feel." The iPod wasn't a better product than the Zune. It was about freedom of movement, self-expression, your library in your hands. The Zune didn't market what having a Zune did for you. Same hardware category, radically different perception architecture.

I created "stay connected, even when you're apart" for Simply Smart Home's digital frames. My team didn't like it — partly because they didn't think of it, partly because they couldn't think of anything in the same league. Then campaigns with the new tagline outperformed everything else. Two years later, Aura Frames was using nearly identical messaging. When your competitor steals your tagline, you found the emotional core.

**In practice:**
- Find the emotional core: what does the user FEEL after using this product? Not what it does — what it does for them.
- Audit every headline: features or outcomes? "WiFi-enabled digital frame" vs. "Stay connected, even when you're apart."
- Compare surveys vs. analytics. The gap is where perception bias hides.
- Protect the design from stakeholder preferences. They're "situational experts" — they recognize good design from the end-user perspective, which is fair. But recognizing a great song on the radio isn't the same as knowing how to make one.

**How I know it's failing:** Marketing focuses on features. Internal stakeholders love the design, but customers don't buy. Copy describes what the product does, not why you'd want it.

### Layer 4: Decision Architecture

> "They're hunters looking for prey, and it's my job to make a trail."

Every design is a choice architecture. There is no neutral presentation of options. Defaults, framing, order, visibility — all of it shapes behavior. This builds on Thaler & Sunstein's work (2008), though a Bayesian re-analysis suggests nudge effects may be near-zero after correcting for publication bias (Maier et al., 2022). The structural principles — defaults matter, framing matters, fewer options at decision points reduce paralysis — remain well-supported.

**Context qualifier on "fewer options":** This principle applies strongest at high-stakes single decisions, unfamiliar purchase choices, and low-expertise contexts. It does NOT apply in expert tool palettes, browse/reference interfaces, or workspace contexts — where option reduction actively harms productivity. A meta-analysis found the choice overload effect near-zero across many conditions (Scheibehenne, Greifeneder & Todd, 2010). The operative principle is: reduce choices *at decision points*, not choices everywhere.

This is the final layer because it requires everything below it to be functional. A perfectly designed trail doesn't help if the visitor is overloaded (Foundation), doesn't trust the site (Layer 1), finds the presentation hard to process (Layer 2), or doesn't connect with the messaging (Layer 3).

**In practice:**
- Navigation reflects user goals, not your org chart. Users think in problems ("Pricing," "How it Works"), not departments ("Solutions," "Resources").
- One primary CTA per viewport section. Too many buttons = choice paralysis. Zero buttons = dead end.
- Show the premium option first — anchoring makes the standard feel like a deal.
- CTAs at natural decision points: after value is established, after social proof, after the price. Not before.

**How I know it's failing:** Users reach for the search bar instead of navigation. Main action is below the fold or lost in noise. No clear default option. "What should I click?" feedback. High conversion but low retention — dark-pattern smell. Experts frustrated by hidden or reduced tooling — misapplied L4 to an expert context where option density is a feature, not a bug.

**Simply Smart Home homepage:** Before: "didn't answer any of the user's questions, goals, or form a good impression." Navigation reflected the company structure. After: rebuilt as a trail. Emotional hook → understands the product → social proof → clear path to purchase. Users found what they wanted instead of bouncing to alternatives.

## The Diagnostic

Here's how I actually diagnose a site. It's not a checklist — it's a process.

### Step 1: Feel (Pre-Verbal)

I arrive at the page and let the emotional response fire before Wernicke's area translates it into words. "This makes me feel X." The feeling comes before the language. System 1 before System 2.

My ADHD sensitivity is actually an advantage here — I feel cognitive load and friction more acutely than most users. The emotional response IS the diagnostic instrument.

### Step 2: Unpack

"Why does this feel X?" Now I'm looking for the systematic rule that's broken. Common patterns:

- Don't overload
- Be specific
- Get to your point
- Signal-to-noise ratio
- Everything has a purpose
- This should feel X — does it?

### Step 3: Diagnose

Data red flags map to layers:

| Signal | Likely Layer |
|--------|-------------|
| High bounce + low time-on-site | Layer 1: First impression |
| High time + low conversion | Layer 4: Decision architecture (or trust) |
| High add-to-cart + low checkout | Foundation: Cognitive load (or price perception) |
| Good desktop + poor mobile | Layer 2: Processing fluency |
| Stated behavior ≠ actual behavior | Layer 3: Perception bias |

### Step 4: Prescribe

Fix the lowest failing layer first. Always. Fixing decision architecture when the first impression is broken is like optimizing a trail that nobody enters.

The difference between a C- and an A+ is usually typography, spacing, and styling — small systematic tweaks that take something from 90% done to fully resolved. Those last 5-10% changes are disproportionately impactful because processing fluency compounds.

### The 5-Minute Perception Audit

Five quick tests, one per layer:

1. **Path Count (Cognitive Load)** — Count every decision from landing to purchase. Compare to competitors. Higher count = losing to friction.
2. **5-Second Test (First Impression)** — Show the homepage to someone for 5 seconds. Can they tell you what the company does, how it made them feel, and whether they'd trust it with their credit card?
3. **Squint Test (Processing Fluency)** — Blur your eyes. What stands out? If everything looks equally important, nothing is.
4. **Analytics Gap (Perception Bias)** — Compare what users say vs. what they do. The gap is where problems hide.
5. **Trail Test (Decision Architecture)** — Incognito mode, first-time visitor. Hunt for the core offer. Three clicks or search bar?

## The Generative Protocol

The Diagnostic decodes failures. The Generative Protocol derives solutions. They're complementary — diagnostic tells you what's broken, generative tells you what to build. The distinction matters because evaluation and generation are different cognitive operations. Evaluating a proposal against PFD catches surface violations. Deriving a solution bottom-up from PFD's constraints surfaces requirements that no existing proposal addresses.

**Rule Zero:** Do NOT propose any solution until all 5 layers are analyzed and requirements accumulated. Solution-first thinking contaminates the requirement space — you stop seeing what the layers demand and start seeing what the solution provides.

### Step 1 — State the Design Problem

What is the user trying to do? What prevents them? What do they experience? Do NOT describe the problem as missing features or comparisons to other tools. "Users can't find the checkout" is a design problem. "We need a sidebar" is a solution masquerading as a problem.

### Step 2 — Work Each Layer Bottom-Up

For each layer (Foundation → L1 → L2 → L3 → L4):

a. State the constraint (the biological/psychological hard limit)
b. State the violation (how the current design fails this constraint)
c. Derive the REQUIREMENT — what the design MUST do, not "should"
d. Label it R[n]

### Step 3 — Accumulate Requirements

List R1–R5. All non-negotiable. The solution must satisfy ALL simultaneously. When requirements conflict, lower-layer requirements win — the dependency stack is the tiebreaker.

### Step 4 — Derive the Solution

What satisfies R1 AND R2 AND R3 AND R4 AND R5? If no single intervention covers all five, what minimal set does? The solution emerges from the constraint intersection, not from brainstorming.

### Step 5 — Test Against Proposals

If proposals already exist, check each against the full requirement set. Note which requirements each proposal fails. This is where the gap between "sounds good" and "satisfies all constraints" becomes visible.

### Step 6 — Identify the Gap

Requirements that no proposal satisfies = where the non-obvious solution lives. This is the payoff of bottom-up derivation: it surfaces the requirement that everyone missed because they were thinking in solutions.

## The Gate

Run before shipping. Each layer is pass/fail. If any layer below L4 fails, do not ship — downstream layers cannot compensate for upstream failures. This is the dependency stack applied as a quality gate.

| Layer | Gate Question | Pass Condition |
|---|---|---|
| L0 | Can a first-time user complete the primary task without getting stuck? | Zero "where do I click?" moments on test run |
| L1 | Does the first impression activate the right emotion and communicate the thesis? | 5-second test: users can state purpose + feel trust |
| L2 | Is every visual and verbal element consistent with the brand and price point? | Squint test passes. No rogue fonts, colors, or spacing. |
| L3 | Does the design speak to what users *respond to*, not what they *say*? | Copy leads with outcomes, not features. Analytics aligns. |
| L4 | Does the trail lead to conversion without manipulation? | Trail test: core offer ≤ 3 clicks. Passes all three ethical tests. |

### Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| Solution-first ("we need a sidebar") | Contaminates requirement space |
| Competitive copying ("Miro does X") | Domain-driven, not perception-driven |
| Engineering-first ("what's fastest?") | Optimizes effort, not perception |
| Checklist mode (evaluating post-hoc) | Refines but doesn't generate |
| Skipping layers | Dependency stack violation — upstream failures block downstream |
| Soft requirements ("should" not "must") | Lets solutions slide past gaps |

## The Automated Gate

PFD's dependency stack maps to a testable quality pipeline. Not all layers are equally automatable — but the layers that are catch the highest-volume failures before human judgment enters. The pipeline runs bottom-up, same as the framework: a failing L0 check makes L4 evaluation meaningless.

### Layer Automation Spectrum

| Layer | Automation Potential | What Machines Catch | What Humans Catch |
|---|---|---|---|
| L0 — Cognitive Load | **High** | Form field counts, click depth to primary action, decision-point density per page, navigation depth | "Does this *feel* overwhelming?" |
| L1 — First Impression | **Medium** | Lighthouse LCP/CLS/FCP, visual complexity metrics, image resolution, above-fold content completeness | "Does this *feel* trustworthy?" |
| L2 — Processing Fluency | **Highest** | Design token consistency, off-system values, font/color audits, spacing rhythm validation, cross-page brand consistency | "Does this *feel* coherent?" |
| L3 — Perception Bias | **Low** | Feature-vs-outcome language ratio (NLP-assisted), survey-analytics gap flagging | Empathy modeling, emotional core identification |
| L4 — Decision Architecture | **Medium** | CTA count per viewport, trail depth, primary action visibility/contrast, navigation structure | "Does the trail *feel* natural?" |

### Integration Points

- **Design tokens → L2 linting.** Every off-token value in a codebase is a processing fluency violation — but not all violations are equal. Near-miss deviations (a color 3-5% off the token value) impose *more* perceptual cost than far-misses (a completely different color), because human color perception is non-additive: the perceptual cost curve is steepest for small differences and compresses for large ones (Bujack et al., 2022). A CSS audit that flags non-token colors, spacing values, or font sizes catches L2 drift before it compounds. Near-miss values should be flagged as higher-severity than far-miss values — a slightly-off brand blue is more disruptive than a clearly different accent color. This is the most automatable layer because token-level consistency is binary — a value either matches the design system or it doesn't. Perceptual consistency (whether the page *feels* coherent) is harder to automate, but catching every off-system value is a reliable proxy.
- **Core Web Vitals → L1 proxy.** LCP, CLS, and FCP correlate with perceived speed and polish. A slow site fails the first impression before any visual design is evaluated. Performance metrics are an indirect but reliable L1 gate.
- **Click-path analysis → L0 + L4.** Automated crawlers can map every path from landing to conversion. Path count is an L0 metric (cognitive load). Path clarity — whether the trail has dead ends, loops, or competing branches — is an L4 metric (decision architecture).
- **Copy analysis → L3 assist.** NLP can flag feature-heavy vs. outcome-heavy language ratios. It can surface pages where every headline describes *what the product does* rather than *what having the product feels like*. It can't determine the emotional core — but it can flag when copy is all specs and no resonance.

### What Automation Cannot Replace

The pre-verbal "feel" (Diagnostic Step 1). The empathy modeling that determines "for whom." The activation point judgment — knowing which moment in the experience should break autopilot and demand attention. These are human pattern-recognition at the practitioner level — the 325-project library that runs automatically after years of deliberate practice. Automation handles the infrastructure checks; the practitioner handles the music.

The split is clean: machines catch **consistency** (L2), **complexity** (L0), and **structure** (L4). Humans catch **resonance** (L1, L3) and **feel** (all layers). A mature design practice uses both — automated gates as pre-flight, human gates as final sign-off.

## The Communication Hierarchy (Practitioner Model)

Music, humor, and stories — three modalities for changing perception, ranked by power. This ranking is based on 15 years of applied work and supported by converging evidence from music cognition, narrative psychology, and humor research. Each level is independently grounded in published literature. The unified hierarchy is my practitioner synthesis — it has not been tested as a single model.

### Music (Most Powerful)

Music communicates emotion, how to think and feel, and momentum — and it bypasses rational processing entirely. Koelsch (2014) showed that music modulates subcortical emotion structures (amygdala, nucleus accumbens, hippocampus) rapidly and potentially prior to conscious evaluation. Juslin & Västfjäll (2008) identified six mechanisms for music-evoked emotion, the majority of which bypass conscious processing: brainstem reflexes, evaluative conditioning, emotional contagion. Huron (2006) mapped this as the ITPRA theory — Imagination, Tension, Prediction, Reaction, Appraisal — where only appraisal is fully conscious. In design, this shows up as typography rhythm, spacing cadence, visual flow, animation timing, scroll momentum. The "music" of a page is what controls pacing and emotional state.

When someone has unconscious mastery of an instrument, they stop thinking about how things are done and focus on how the music makes you feel. After 15 years of applied work, that's where I am. The methodology runs automatically. The focus is on the outcome.

Singing down a scale and pausing before the last note — everyone knows what comes next. The pattern demands completion. That's what a well-designed page feels like: each section creates the expectation that naturally resolves in the next. The CTA is the last note of the scale.

### Humor (Second)

Jokes change how people feel about a thing by pointing out absurdity and giving permission to think without rigidity. A joke is: setup creates an expectation, the punch reveals a pattern that either aligns with your internal self-image ("I'd do that!") or doesn't ("I'd never do that!"), and the anxiety between setup and punch amplifies the payoff. Laughter is the anxiety being released.

In design: pattern interrupts, unexpected delight, personality in copy, moments where the experience subverts expectation in a satisfying way. The "rule of eventually" from improv — "that's horri-bly... good!" — is a micro-version of this.

Three comedy escalation patterns that map directly to UX:
- **Linear repetition:** Same intensity, building familiarity (consistent brand messaging)
- **Sequential escalation:** Building momentum toward a peak (marketing funnel narrative)
- **Exponential escalation:** Each beat dramatically amplifies the last (hero → social proof → testimonial → CTA)

### Stories (Third)

Stories are a deeply rooted way we simulate experiences without having to live them. Green & Brock (2000) demonstrated "narrative transportation" — absorption into a story produces belief changes consistent with the narrative. Zak (2015) proposes that dramatic narrative arcs are associated with cortisol release (heightened attention) and oxytocin release (empathy/connection), with elevated oxytocin correlating with prosocial behavior. **Important caveat:** the intranasal oxytocin literature has severe methodological problems — Mierop et al. (2020) found an estimated 90% false positive rate across the field, and Zak's studies used small, unpreregistered samples. The specific neurochemical mechanism should be considered undemonstrated. The behavioral finding — that stories change beliefs and attitudes — does not depend on Zak. Green & Brock (2000) demonstrated narrative transportation robustly, and two meta-analyses confirm the broader pattern: van Laer et al. (2014) across 132 effect sizes, and Braddock & Dillard (2016) across narrative persuasion studies. Stories work. The oxytocin explanation of *why* they work is not settled science. But the story isn't the events — it's how someone changes from A to B. That's directly applicable to a website: the user is the protagonist. "I have a problem" → "This solves my problem." The hero section is the inciting incident. The CTA is the climax. Testimonials are supporting characters who've already completed the journey.

## Websites as Relationships

Users evaluate websites the same way they evaluate people. Same unconscious process, same red and green flags. Gray, Gray & Wegner's (2007) two dimensions of mind perception — **Agency** (competence, capacity to act) and **Experience** (empathy, capacity to care) — map directly onto how users judge websites:

| Relationship Red Flag | Website Equivalent | Mind Perception Dimension |
|---|---|---|
| Doesn't take appearance seriously | Low-quality design, broken layouts | Low Agency (incompetent) |
| Lack of confidence | Hedging language, unclear value proposition | Low Agency |
| Too verbose / won't get to the point | Walls of text, burying the lead | Low Agency |
| Non-specific / vague promises | Generic claims, no specifics, no proof | Low Agency + Low Experience |
| Too good to be true | Over-promising without social proof | Suspicious Agency (ulterior motive) |
| Acts like you're lucky to be there | Self-centered messaging, no empathy for user needs | Low Experience (doesn't care about you) |

**The trust-killer that operates before anyone reads a word: disrespect.** A site that doesn't treat users with respect — their time, their intelligence, their needs — triggers the same rejection as a person who acts like you're lucky to interact with them.

How seriously a business takes their digital presentation is a direct indicator of how seriously they'll take interacting with you. That's expectation setting. And it's the first thing users perceive — before a single word is read.

## The Responsibility

If we're engineers of thoughts and emotions, we need a Hippocratic oath. The design industry doesn't have one — the ACM Code of Ethics exists but nobody outside academia reads it. Here's the one I operate by.

**The distinction:** The designer controls the perception layer — presentation, UX, visual hierarchy, emotional messaging, activation points. The organization controls the value layer — product quality, pricing integrity, fulfillment, service. PFD's ethical mandate is to **align perception with reality.**

When the product is good and the perception is bad — that's the Simply Smart Home case — PFD closes the gap upward, bringing perception up to match genuine value. That's not manipulation. That's correction. The violation occurs in the opposite direction: inflating perception above reality.

**The Three-Part Ethical Test:**

1. **The Alignment Test:** Does this design bring perception closer to reality, or further from it? (Berdichevsky & Neuenschwander, 1999)
2. **The Sincerity Test:** If the user fully understood what this design choice does, would they feel served or exploited? Consumers don't object to persuasion — they object to persuasion perceived as insincere (Friestad & Wright, 1994; Campbell & Kirmani, 2000).
3. **The Golden Rule:** Would I, as the designer, consent to being influenced by this technique if I were the user?

A design that passes all three tests is a better design — not just ethically, but commercially. It builds trust, reduces what Friestad & Wright call the "persuasion knowledge" response (the user's BS detector), and creates long-term relationships instead of one-time conversions. Dark patterns (Brignull, 2010; Gray et al., 2018) — nagging, sneaking, forced action — are the cheap laughs of design. They convert short-term and destroy trust. PFD is the expensive laugh: genuine emotional resonance that compounds.

> "Perception-First Design removes perception barriers between users and genuine value. It does not create the perception of value where none exists. The designer is responsible for the perception layer; the organization is responsible for the value layer. When these diverge, the designer's obligation is to the user."

This isn't theoretical for me. I helped start QTBIPOC.Design (a nonprofit, 5.5 years and counting) — we've refused funding from Fortune 500 companies because of value misalignment. My business model — Build → Host → Retain → Full-time, no one-off builds without hosting — naturally filters out clients who want perception inflated beyond reality. They wouldn't commit to a long-term relationship where the gap would show. Ethics isn't a constraint on the work. It's a quality criterion.

**Operational ethics vs. structural ethics:** The three tests above are *operational* ethics — executable by one designer in a single review session. They handle decision-making at the design level. The business model is *structural* ethics — it enforces alignment systemically, regardless of any individual practitioner's values or judgment calls. Operational ethics assumes good values; structural ethics enforces them. Both matter, but at different scales. A framework that relies only on operational ethics fails when the practitioner is tired, rushed, or incentivized to cut corners. A framework that relies only on structural ethics fails when a novel situation falls outside the model's rules. PFD uses both.

**Relationship to Value Sensitive Design:** PFD's ethical framework sits within the broader HCI tradition of Value Sensitive Design (Friedman, Kahn & Borning, 2002) — the systematic study of how technology design reflects, encodes, and shapes human values across all stakeholders. Knobel & Bowker (2011) extended this line of inquiry to ask how values get *embedded* into designed systems — not just studied after the fact, but actively inscribed through design decisions that privilege certain stakeholders, workflows, and outcomes over others. PFD's perception layer is exactly such an inscription point: every visual hierarchy, every default, every activation point encodes a judgment about whose attention matters and what they should do with it. The structural parallel runs deeper than ethics: VSD's tripartite methodology requires conceptual, empirical, and technical investigations. PFD's 5-layer analysis is a form of conceptual investigation (what perceptual constraints are at stake?). The accumulated client work constitutes empirical investigation (how do users actually experience these constraints?). The diagnostic protocol is technical investigation (how does this specific design encode or violate them?). PFD arrived at this structure through practice, not through VSD's literature — the convergence suggests the underlying logic is sound.

Where VSD asks broad questions about which values a system embeds and for whom, PFD's ethical mandate is narrower and operational: does this specific design bring perception closer to reality, for this user, in this context? That narrowing is intentional — an executable practitioner framework needs tests you can actually run on a Tuesday afternoon. The broader VSD question — what values does "conversion" itself encode, and whose interests does that serve? — is worth holding alongside the operational tests. PFD doesn't resolve it. It just refuses to make it worse.

### What PFD Doesn't See

Every framework has blind spots — things it makes visible and things it occludes. Knobel's concept of **ontic occlusion** (how one representation of reality blocks another from being seen) applies to PFD itself. Naming these isn't a weakness. It's a quality criterion.

**Structural power dynamics.** PFD's ethical test says "align perception with reality." But it doesn't interrogate who decides what "reality" is. When SSH's product page undersold a good product, the "reality" was clear — the product was better than the perception. But what about a company whose product is mediocre and whose "reality" is genuinely ambiguous? The alignment test assumes a knowable ground truth. In practice, "reality" is contested, and the designer choosing which reality to align with is itself a power move that PFD doesn't surface.

**Individual vs. collective.** PFD models one user's cognitive journey through the mental waterfall. It doesn't model what happens when thousands of users are conducted through the same waterfall simultaneously. Individual perception optimization can produce collective harms — engagement loops, attention extraction at scale, homogenization of choice. PFD's unit of analysis is the user. The effects that emerge at population scale are outside its frame.

**The fluency trap.** Processing fluency is L2's core mechanism: "if it's easy to process, it feels true." That mechanism is value-neutral. It works for honest brands and dishonest ones. A fluent lie is more persuasive than a disfluent truth. PFD's ethical tests catch *deliberate* manipulation — but they don't address the structural problem: by optimizing for fluency, PFD makes it harder for users to engage the critical evaluation that would catch deception. The very thing that makes PFD effective (reducing prediction error, keeping users in autopilot through the desired path) is the same thing that can prevent them from stopping to question. This is ontic occlusion baked into the mechanism.

**The designer's own perception biases.** PFD's diagnostic starts with "Feel" — the designer's pre-verbal emotional response as the primary instrument. But that instrument is calibrated by the designer's own culture, class, neurotype, and aesthetic history. I name my neurodivergence as an advantage (heightened sensitivity to friction). But heightened sensitivity to friction could also mean under-sensitivity to other things — social warmth cues, cultural signifiers outside my experience, accessibility needs I don't personally feel. The diagnostic instrument has its own biases, and PFD doesn't yet have a protocol for calibrating them.

## The Results

### Simply Smart Home

**The problem:** Template site. No unified branding. Feature-focused marketing. Premium price point with a presentation that said "knockoff."

**Every layer was failing:**
1. Site didn't answer basic user questions (Cognitive Load)
2. No faces, no warmth, no emotional context (First Impression)
3. Template at a premium price — quality/price mismatch (Processing Fluency)
4. Feature specs instead of emotional connection (Perception Bias)
5. Homepage structured around company, not user goals (Decision Architecture)

**What I did:** Unified brand system. Emotional messaging — "stay connected, even when you're apart." Hero sections with faces showing the product in use. Consistent collateral across every touchpoint. Navigation redesigned around user goals. 4-year systematic brand infrastructure build.

I created "smart home decor" as a feeling and category for what was actually $20-40 tablets with plastic casings sold for $100-200. Perceived value meant more than anything else sales or marketing tried to do to increase sales.

**Results:**
- Revenue tripled within a year
- Disney licensing, Costco pallets, Walmart shelf space
- Competitor stole the tagline two years later

The product was good — the perception wasn't. I aligned perception with reality. The fence-sitters — people who wanted the product but were stopped by perception barriers — started buying. The product didn't change. The perception did. I doubt sales would have reached half of that otherwise.

## What's Teachable

### If I Had 3 Hours with Business Owners

**Hour 1: Business Design.** Your business IS a design. It's a series of feedback loops between your vertical, your users, and your staff. Processes are the core of business and product design. Loops between everyone involved are the iterative engine. I'd give them the tools to see their business as a system and the language to identify what they can change.

**Hour 2: People as Research.** Your staff and clients are your greatest resources — they know the product, the workflow, the pain points better than anyone. Use them as primary qualitative research. And here's the shift: design problem-solving is really just a lens of empathy you see things through. "For whom is this?" gives you directionality that frameworks and best practices can't.

**Hour 3: Tactics.** Now that you see your business as a design and your people as your data, here's what to do. The 5-Minute Perception Audit. The dependency stack. The red flags. Application, communication, process.

### If I Had 3 Hours with Junior Designers

This is about desired outcomes — what's *best*, not what's right or wrong. And most importantly, it's not about you. Don't get attached to a design or solution. We're not decorators. We're **engineers of thoughts and emotions.**

The universal modality is sensory input with what we're familiar with. We literally use words like rough, coarse, soft, slippery, cold to describe sounds, visuals, balance — not just touch. The brain processes everything through embodied sensory metaphors.

And here's what nobody tells you in school: **design is 1/3 design, 1/3 communication, and 1/3 process/administration.** The design part is what you love. The communication part is how you get stakeholders to let you do good work. The process part is how the work actually ships.

### The Teachability Line

The **lens** — empathy, for-whom, design-as-problem-solving — is fully teachable. Give anyone that lens and they get directionality.

The **pattern library** — hundreds of projects of instant recognition, knowing what tactics to use without research — takes years. No shortcut.

The **sensitivity** — neurodivergent tuning fork for bad design — may not be teachable at all. But the diagnostic **process** (Feel → Unpack → Diagnose → Prescribe) can be taught as a substitute.

## The Improv Rules

My design practice runs on principles I learned from improv classes. These aren't metaphors — they're operational tools.

| Improv Rule | How I Use It in Design |
|---|---|
| **Yes, and** | Accept what exists and build on it. Don't throw everything out when something feels wrong. Brick by brick, not castle at a time. |
| **(Listen) × (Act + React)** | The formula for being in the moment. If you're not listening, you multiply everything by zero. Same for users — if they're not attending, nothing lands. |
| **"If this is true, what else is true?"** | One insight cascades. If this demographic does X, what else follows? If this is the brand voice here, it should be everywhere. |
| **"What comes before / what comes after?"** | User journey thinking. What preceded this page? What follows? |
| **"It's not for you, it's for them"** | Protect the design from stakeholder preferences. Design for the target's autopilot, not your comfort. |
| **Rule of eventually** | "That's horri-bly... good!" — subvert expectations mid-flow for engagement. |
| **Hold topics and return** | Progressive disclosure. Introduce a concept, develop it elsewhere, callback at the conversion point. |
| **"What's familiar to you is novel to others"** | You know your product inside out. Your users don't. Design for their first encounter, not your hundredth. |

Telling clients they're correct while disagreeing with them and offering solutions — that's yes-and in practice. Getting stakeholders to reason from the context of who it's for instead of "can we have a button here?" — that's the lens of empathy in action. It's like hearing a great song on the radio: recognizing quality is fair, but recognizing quality isn't the same as knowing what goes into making it.

## Open Questions

These are genuinely unresolved. A living methodology names its edges.

**Cultural generalizability.** The evidence base is predominantly WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic). The underlying cognitive architecture — predictive processing, working memory limits, first-impression formation — is expected to generalize. But the *parameters* (which visual patterns signal trust, what counts as "too much" cognitive load, how fluency maps to credibility) are culturally shaped. PFD has been applied in Western e-commerce and entertainment. How the 5-layer dependency order holds in collectivist markets, non-Latin typographic traditions, or markets with fundamentally different trust signals is an open question.

**Measurement.** PFD lacks a validated measurement instrument. The 5-Minute Perception Audit is a practitioner heuristic — useful, fast, and directionally correct, but without inter-rater reliability data. A diagnostic tool where two PFD practitioners independently audit the same site and converge on the same layer diagnosis would strengthen the methodology from "expert intuition with a framework" to "teachable, repeatable diagnostic." This is the gap between a clinical art and a clinical science.

**Temporal dynamics.** PFD describes the first encounter well — activation, first impression, mental waterfall. But user relationships with products evolve. At visit 100, the mental waterfall is different. Prediction errors that activated on first visit are now expected. Fluency that felt trustworthy can become invisible (users stop noticing). Does the 5-layer stack apply the same way to retention and long-term engagement as it does to acquisition? The current framework is weighted toward first contact.

**Accessibility as justice vs. fluency.** PFD frames accessibility through L0 (cognitive load reduction) and L2 (processing fluency). This produces good accessible design in practice. But disability justice scholars argue accessibility is a *rights* issue, not an optimization target. Framing it through "fluency" risks treating accessibility as something you optimize when the ROI justifies it, rather than something you guarantee because people deserve it. The framework's utilitarian lens may not fully serve this.

**AI-generated design.** As AI tools generate more design artifacts, PFD's emphasis on embodied sensitivity — the "neurodivergent tuning fork," the pre-verbal Feel step — becomes harder to operationalize at scale. Can PFD's diagnostic be automated? Can an AI run the Feel step? If not, the methodology has a ceiling on how far it scales beyond the individual practitioner. If yes, what's lost when the diagnostic instrument is no longer a human body?

## The Mindset

### "Design is intentionality from our minds into reality."

That's what this is. Not art — art is whatever whoever wants to feel or think it is. Design is intentional. We create the future and the things we want to exist from our minds. That's stupidly profound when you sit with it.

If you can imagine an outcome, you can design your way towards it with intentionality and context making it more likely to get the outcome you imagine.

### "Design is a lens of empathy to solve with."

For whom. Always for whom. When you have that lens, directionality becomes clear. Without it, you're decorating. With it, you're engineering outcomes.

### "Engineers of thoughts and emotions."

That's the job. Not "web designer." Not "UX." We engineer how people think and feel about experiences. Society needs us to imagine what the future is and drag us there through the chaos of reality.

### "This could be a lot worse. But it could also be a lot better."

That's the mindset shift. Everyone's been designing their whole lives — choosing what to wear, how to arrange their desk, how to phrase an email. The framework just makes it intentional.

### "Being in the moment is magical. Designing the moments you'll step into is life-changing."

**Note on provenance:** Several PFD concepts were independently developed through embodied practice (improv performance, security work, 15 years of client work) before encountering the corresponding literature. The improv notebook (2012-2013) contains a practitioner articulation of the core predictive processing operation — "comparing mental representation with reality" — that predates Clark's landmark 2013 paper. PFD is a theoretical synthesis: existing cognitive science organized into an actionable design framework through 15 years of applied work.

**Note on methodology:** This document was formalized with AI assistance (Claude, Anthropic). The underlying methodology, case studies, and practitioner insights are the author's own work developed over 15 years. AI was used for: structuring the write-up, identifying and linking relevant literature, iterative refinement, and multi-perspective analysis through deliberate AI persona brainstorming sessions (structured analytical lenses with assigned domain specializations — accessibility, cartography, environmental psychology, etc.). These persona-driven sessions are a legitimate part of the methodology, not a failure mode — they surface insights from analytical frames the author wouldn't naturally default to. Citations were separately audited for accuracy — that audit caught one fabricated citation (removed), one unverified citation (verified as real after search), and several overstated claims (hedged). An earlier version of the document header also described the persona analyses as "3 expert reviews" without clarifying they were AI-driven — that mislabeling has been corrected. The framework's ideas predate the AI assistance; the formalization into this document does not.

**Note on generalizability:** Most empirical benchmarks cited here (50ms judgments, 3-5 chunks, processing fluency effects) were measured primarily on Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic (WEIRD) samples (Henrich, Heine & Norenzayan, 2010). The cognitive architecture is expected to be universal; specific parameter values may vary across populations.

## Appendix C: Glossary

- **Perception barrier** — An unconscious objection that prevents conversion. Not a product, price, or demand problem. A problem with how the experience is perceived.
- **Fence-sitter** — Someone with intent who doesn't convert due to perception barriers.
- **Mental waterfall** — The conducted sequence of thoughts and emotions a user experiences from first impression through conversion.
- **The activation point** — The moment a user switches from autopilot to active engagement. Usually the first impression / hero section.
- **The Listen Multiplier** — (Listen) × (Act + React). If the user isn't listening, everything else multiplies by zero.
- **Cognitive load** — Total mental effort required to use an interface. Intrinsic (task) + extraneous (design).
- **Processing fluency** — Subjective ease of processing information. Higher fluency = more positive evaluations across truth, trust, and beauty.
- **Perception bias** — Systematic gap between what users say, what stakeholders believe, and what actually happens.
- **Decision architecture** — The structure of choices as presented. Every structure is a nudge. There is no neutral arrangement.
- **Empathy modeling** — Embodying the traits and context of a target demographic to get directionality on design decisions.
- **The hardware layer** — Shared cognitive architecture: attention, memory, autopilot, mind perception, predictive processing. Universal structure, variable parameters (processing speed, acuity decline with age). Design for the floor.
- **The software layer** — Cultural conventions: style preferences, expectations, literacy, aspirational identity. Varies by audience.
- **Mind perception** — Users construct lightweight models of competence (Agency) and care (Experience) for websites, brands, and products — the same dimensions they use to evaluate people (Gray, Gray & Wegner, 2007). Not full Theory of Mind, not just social heuristics. Enough to pass or fail trust evaluation.
- **Predictive processing** — The brain constantly generates predictions about incoming input. Matched predictions = autopilot (fluency). Violated predictions = attention fires (activation). The theoretical backbone of PFD (Clark, 2013; Friston, 2010).
- **Mental prosthesis** — Scaffolding for cognitive functions and limitations. Good design IS prosthesis for the brain's limited bandwidth. The curb cut effect: what you build for accessibility helps everyone.
