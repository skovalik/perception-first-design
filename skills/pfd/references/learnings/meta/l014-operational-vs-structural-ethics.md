# Learning 14: PFD Ethics Are Operational, Not Structural

**Insight:** PFD's three ethical tests (Alignment, Sincerity, Golden Rule) are *operational* ethics — executable by one designer in a single review session. They don't constitute *structural* ethics in the Value Sensitive Design sense (systematic processes that produce good outcomes regardless of individual practitioner values). The business model (Build → Host → Retain) IS structural ethics — long-term relationship accountability enforces alignment better than any framework clause could. These operate at different scales and both matter: operational tests for decision-making, structural incentives for accountability. Practical implication: when a client, collaborator, or critic raises "but what stops someone from misusing PFD?" — the answer is the business model, not the tests. The tests assume good values; the model enforces them. When PFD scales beyond solo practice (certification, team use), structural ethics needs to be baked into the process, not just carried by the practitioner. VSD (Friedman, Kahn & Borning, 2002) is the academic tradition this sits within; PFD's ethical scope is deliberately narrower and more operational.
